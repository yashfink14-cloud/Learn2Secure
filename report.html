Cybercrime and Cybersecurity Awareness Survey Report 📊

**Date of Report:** September 15, 2025

Objectives

The primary objective of this survey was to assess the level of awareness and understanding of cybercrime and cybersecurity practices among a targeted demographic. The survey aimed to gather data on how individuals perceive cyber threats, their knowledge of safe online behavior, and their past experiences with cyberattacks. The findings will be used to identify knowledge gaps and inform future educational initiatives.

Methodology

This report is based on the responses from an online survey conducted with 217 participants. The survey was divided into two main sections: one focusing on cybercrime awareness and the other on cybersecurity practices. The data presented herein is a direct representation of the responses collected.

Analysis

The survey results reveal a strong baseline of awareness regarding cybercrime and cybersecurity among participants. The majority of respondents fall within the 18-25 age group and are students, indicating that this demographic has a high level of engagement with digital platforms.

Cybercrime Awareness

A significant majority of respondents, 95.9%, have heard of cybercrime, and 98.2% know the correct first step to take in case of cyber fraud: reporting it to the official portal or a helpline number. Most participants correctly identified phishing emails as the most common method for hackers to steal information, with 89.4% selecting this option. However, there is a small gap in knowledge, with some individuals not being able to identify common examples of cybercrime or financial fraud..

Cybersecurity Practices

While the awareness of threats is high, the practices section shows some areas for improvement. A large portion of respondents stated they update their passwords only every 3-6 months (48.4%), while a combined 32.7% do so either once a year or rarely/never. Additionally, nearly half of the participants (46.5%) sometimes use the same password for multiple accounts, and another 10.6% always do. Concerningly, over a third of respondents (39.2%) do not use antivirus or firewall software on their devices.

Conclusion

The survey clearly demonstrates that while the public is generally aware of cybercrime and understands how to react to it, there is a significant opportunity to improve proactive cybersecurity habits. Future educational campaigns should focus on actionable steps, such as encouraging the use of unique passwords, password managers, and antivirus software. By promoting these simple, effective habits, we can help bridge the gap between awareness and practice, leading to a more secure digital community.
